2. Methods

Data source, classification, and limitations

Data source

The Anthropic Economic Index V4, released January 2026, contains two samples of one million conversations each: one from Claude.ai (consumer) and one from first-party API usage (developer). Both were drawn from the week of 13–20 November 2025.

This analysis primarily uses the Claude.ai sample. The API sample is used only for comparison in Section 3.6 of the Results. The full dataset is publicly available on HuggingFace.

How Clio classifies conversations

Anthropic's Clio tool processes each conversation through several classification steps:

  • Task matching. Each conversation is mapped to one or more O*NET occupational tasks. These tasks are standardised descriptions of work activities (e.g., "prepare course materials such as syllabi, homework assignments, and handouts").
  • Collaboration pattern. Clio classifies the interaction into one of five patterns: directive (human gives instructions, AI executes), task iteration (back-and-forth refinement), learning (human seeks understanding), validation (human checks AI output), and feedback loop (extended exchange with adaptation). Two additional categories, not_classified and none, are excluded from this analysis and the remaining five are renormalised to 100%.
  • Other metrics. Clio also estimates AI autonomy (1–5 scale), task success (binary), required education level, and use case (coursework, work, or personal).

Filtering to education

Education tasks were identified by filtering to SOC Major Group 25 (Educational Instruction and Library Occupations). This yielded 266 of 3,170 total matched tasks, representing 15.2% of total conversation volume.

Weighting and aggregation

All aggregate statistics are volume-weighted using the onet_task_pct field, which represents each task's share of total conversation volume. This means high-traffic tasks (like "assist students with coursework outside class," which accounts for 2.73% of all volume alone) have proportionally greater influence on aggregate figures.

The top 5 education tasks account for approximately 46% of all education conversation volume. This concentration means that aggregate patterns are heavily influenced by a small number of high-traffic tasks.

Renormalisation

Collaboration pattern percentages exclude the not_classified and none categories. The five substantive patterns (directive, task iteration, learning, validation, feedback loop) are renormalised to sum to 100%. This approach makes patterns comparable across tasks but means the reported percentages overstate the share of conversations where a substantive pattern was identified.

Limitations

These limitations are not caveats appended for form. They affect the interpretation of every finding in this report.

Single platform

This data covers Claude.ai only. ChatGPT, Gemini, Copilot, and other tools serve different and likely larger education user populations. Claude.ai's market share, interface design, and model behaviour all shape the patterns observed here. Nothing in this report can be assumed to generalise to other platforms.

One-week temporal window

The sample covers 13–20 November 2025. In the Northern Hemisphere, this is late semester, when assignment loads peak. In parts of the Southern Hemisphere, this is exam season. Usage patterns almost certainly vary across the academic calendar. A January or June sample would likely look different.

Classifier accuracy unknown

Clio's conversation-to-task mapping and collaboration pattern classification are proprietary. No published validation studies, error rates, or inter-rater reliability figures exist for these classifiers. We report Clio's classifications as given, but cannot verify their accuracy.

Interaction pattern does not equal learning intent

This is the most important limitation. A student who types "explain the causes of World War I" is classified as making a directive request. But that student may be learning. A student who iterates on an essay draft through multiple exchanges is classified as task iteration. But write-revise-improve cycles are a core pedagogical process.

The collaboration pattern categories describe conversational structure. They do not measure educational intent or learning outcomes. The Discussion section addresses this interpretation gap in detail.

Baseline errors identified

An independent review of this analysis (see Appendix D) identified that the global collaboration baseline numbers and sub-sector task counts contained errors related to renormalisation methodology and deduplication approach. These have been corrected. The review also flagged the V2-to-V4 trend comparison as methodologically unsound due to different classification models being used across versions. That comparison has been excluded from this report entirely.

Education tasks outside SOC 25

Filtering to SOC Major Group 25 captures teachers, lecturers, librarians, and education support workers. It does not capture education-adjacent roles classified elsewhere, such as school counsellors (SOC 21-1012), education administrators (SOC 11-9030), or training specialists in non-education sectors. The education share reported here is therefore a lower bound of education-related AI usage.

Volume concentration

The top 5 of 266 education tasks account for 46% of education conversation volume. Aggregate statistics are therefore driven disproportionately by a handful of high-traffic tasks. Subsector-level breakdowns partially address this, but the concentration remains a structural feature of the data.

Geographic small-denominator problem

Country-level education share percentages are computed from unknown base volumes. Countries with small Claude.ai user populations can show volatile percentages. A country where 37% of AI conversations are education-related may have a tiny absolute number of conversations. Geographic findings should be treated as indicative, not definitive.
